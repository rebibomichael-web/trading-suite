### [Tesla’s Next Growth Engine Is Under Construction](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PKEEBzt9LnQ)
**Brighter with Herbert** · Jul 16

Tesla's Giga Texas is building out three big projects: a massive Optimus robot factory (roughly 2-2.5 years from real production), the Cortex 2 AI training data center (over 100 megawatts, already about 70% commissioned and partly running), and a new chip fab in Grimes County that's just starting site grading. The chip fab will make Tesla's future AI6 chip in-house (via a partnership with Intel and equipment from ASML), while AI5 is being built by TSMC and Samsung for Optimus. On Cybercab, the analyst argues its stripped-down design (no steering wheel controls, smaller battery, no panoramic glass) gives it a much lower cost per mile than Waymo's retrofitted Jaguar I-Pace, and that Tesla can profit even without surge pricing since owners can feed personal cars into the fleet during peak demand — something Waymo/Uber can't replicate. He estimates Tesla could already manufacture 250k-500k Cybercabs a year if software/regulatory approval catches up, and pegs meaningful revenue from the new Houston Megapack factory at not before H2 2027.

### [Tesla Optimus Faces China’s Biggest Challenge](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U88bNlSzwCE)
**Brighter with Herbert** · Jul 16

Tesla's Optimus and Chinese humanoid robots (like Xiaomi's and Xpeng's Iron robot) are both making rapid progress, though Tesla is deliberately staying quiet about Optimus's real capabilities until it's closer to production, so it's hard to compare them directly right now. On EVs, the speaker pushes back on claims of a brutal China price war, saying Tesla's price cuts are only around 5% (versus reports of BYD cutting up to 40%), and that most Chinese automakers are still gross-margin positive despite heavy reinvestment; BYD's messy 2025 financials were mainly due to a government-forced unwinding of 180-230 day supplier payment deferrals, not fundamental weakness. On FSD, he's now bullish that Hardware 3 (AI3) cars can reach "good enough" full self-driving via the new FSD v14 "light" build, citing a real-world Florida-to-Washington road trip with zero disengagements, though he doubts old HW3 cars will ever join the robotaxi fleet. Finally, he argues against a Tesla-SpaceX merger for now, saying it's premature to value the "merger of equals" until Starship, Optimus, and autonomous ramp progress reveal each company's real worth, and he's skeptical of claims that retail investors were well taken care of in the SpaceX share offering.

### [Fundstrat: What to make of elevated dispersion?](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bm3QSj9sZoY)
**Fundstrat** · Jul 16

Fundstrat's Hardika Singh discusses the CBOE S&P 500 dispersion index hitting its highest level in six years, but says she's not worried about a repeat of the dot-com bubble. She notes chip stocks like Micron, Sandisk, and Intel are down over 30% since the DRAM ETF peaked June 22nd, but those losses are being offset by gains in financials, industrials (Axon Enterprises), Moderna, and Union Pacific. She flags the software-vs-semiconductor dispersion as the widest in 25 years, arguing software names like DoorDash still have strong moats and got hit prematurely, while chips are being beaten down by sentiment. She also highlights that the gap between single-stock volatility and the VIX is the widest ever recorded — a pattern typical ahead of earnings season as traders sell index vol and buy single-stock vol, but one that can turn risky if it unwinds and stock correlations spike together on the downside. Fundstrat's Tom Lee remains bullish longer-term, though he sees some near-term headwinds.
