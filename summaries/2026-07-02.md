Believe in the Broadening Trade? — July 2, 2026

Overview: The committee debated whether the market's rotation away from mega-cap tech and semis into industrials, financials, and regional banks marks a healthy, durable broadening or just a temporary pause before the AI trade reasserts itself. Mega-cap tech stocks staged a comeback this week (Apple, Microsoft, Meta, Amazon all up sharply), while the panel also dug into Caterpillar's AI-driven run, Meta's new cloud ambitions, and software's potential comeback. Brad Gerstner joined to discuss the launch of the Invest America Trump accounts and pushed back on any government equity stakes in AI companies.

- **Josh Brown**: Loves the rotation — tech/comm services/discretionary cooled in June after leading in April-May, while laggards like software catch up; calls it "one of the better tapes we've seen in years." Says Apple (only mega-cap within 5% of highs) is the one to watch in H2 given its upcoming refresh cycle (five new iPhones through '27, foldable, Chinese-made chips). On Meta's cloud pivot, thinks the smart move mirrors SpaceX/xAI leasing compute to Anthropic; still wavering on Meta stock generally — cheap but "tired" of spending without payoff. Best Stocks in the Market pick: Live Nation — deferred revenue up 22% to $6.6B, ticket sales up 11%, 107M tickets sold; sees a "two-handle" before year-end, watching the 50-day ($166) as support and 200-day (~$154) as the line where the trend would break. Final Trade: Netflix — thinks it could bid for NBC Universal/parks business, a better deal than the scrapped WBD deal.
- **Stephanie Link**: Bullish on broad economic strength — consumer spending (6.2% ex-auto retail sales), robust jobless claims/JOLTS, loan growth (H8 data +8%, best in 3 years) — favors industrials, financials, and discretionary into year-end; likes rails/logistics and the FedEx spinoff. Bought more Amazon this week on valuation (23x forward estimates vs. 34x 5-yr avg; 11x EBITDA), citing AWS growth (28%), Prime Day same-store sales (+9.3%), ad business ($70B run rate toward $100B by 2030), and new custom AI chips. On Meta's cloud news, notes it took Amazon/Google/Microsoft a decade to build clouds, so this won't happen overnight; still wants to see better ad-business monetization. Bought more ServiceNow (upgraded to Buy at Guggenheim) — trades at 23x forward vs. 54x 5-yr average, expects 20%+ revenue and earnings growth; argues software's "mission critical" names have decoupled from the SOX selloff (IGV down 11% vs. SOX up 82% YTD). Final Trade: Target — channel checks in LA showed strong product/traffic, same-store sales set to grow mid-single digits at 16x forward estimates.
- **Malcolm Etheridge**: Skeptical of the rotation being "investors finding religion" — points to leveraged semis vehicles (SOXL, Cosby) still showing irrational exuberance, wants to see how earnings season (starting in a couple weeks) plays out before trusting the move. Worried about mega-cap AI spending commitments unwinding, citing Oracle's cautious comments and OpenAI possibly shelving its IPO; predicts OpenAI will be the next to walk back spending commitments as banks push them to clean up financials ahead of a public listing. On Meta's cloud pivot, sees it as a legitimate diversification away from pure ad revenue but compares it skeptically to Intel's foundry push years ago. Final Trade: Alphabet — Google Cloud is one of tech's fastest-growing businesses and AI is improving search.
- **Kevin Simpson**: Bullish on the regional-bank/industrial "ratification" of the economy (KRE and XLI charts look alike) as more meaningful than another leg up in memory chips; calls it more important than AI hype. On Caterpillar (Call of the Day, Truist raised target to $1,218 from $1,043, ~23% upside), estimates ~70% of the move is AI-driven, isn't fazed by Michael Burry's short, hopes for a stock split, and recommends trailing a stop under the rising 50-day (~$913) to protect gains while letting the trend run. Warns that if Meta or another hyperscaler pulls back on capex, Caterpillar (a "third-order" AI beneficiary) would be hit hardest.
- **Featured segment — Halftime Headliner, Brad Gerstner (Altimeter Capital)**: Discussed the Invest America Trump accounts launching Saturday (July 4th weekend), seeded with $1,000 in the S&P 500 for every child born, with corporate contributions pouring in (Micron $250M/1M kids, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Dell family's $6.25B gift, Ray Dalio, Harold Hamm); a joint NYSE/Nasdaq bell-ringing is planned at the White House Monday. On markets, agreed with Josh Brown that the pause in mega-cap/chip momentum is healthy consolidation after Altimeter shifted from "large to medium/small" exposure amid parabolic April-June gains; says the key metric to watch remains Anthropic/OpenAI revenue growth as validation for AI capex, with OpenAI potentially reaching $100B exit revenue and an H2 IPO. On the idea of the government taking equity stakes in AI companies (e.g., a rumored 5% stake in OpenAI), he strongly opposes it as unconstitutional, distinguishing it from the negotiated Intel stake; supports only voluntary equity contributions (echoing Benioff's 1% plan) funneled directly to citizen accounts with no government middleman.
- **Final Trades**: Josh Brown – Netflix (NBC Universal/parks bid speculation); Stephanie Link – Target; Malcolm Etheridge – Alphabet; Kevin Simpson – (no separate final trade segment noted beyond Caterpillar call).
