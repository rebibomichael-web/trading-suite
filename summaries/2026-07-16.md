**The State of the Tech Trade: Your Next Move — 7/16/26**

Overview: Semiconductors and memory stocks tumbled hard while the broader market (S&P, Dow) held near record highs, as the "immaculate rotation" moved money into non-tech sectors, Mag7 laggards like Apple, and financials/healthcare. The panel also dug into IBM's post-earnings plunge as a warning sign on AI-consulting spend, Netflix's earnings setup, and warnings about margin-fueled speculation (especially in Korean leveraged ETFs).

- **Josh Brown**: Owns NVIDIA, Apple, and Netflix. Blasted "thirsty" trading behavior chasing leveraged/0DTE bets on the "it trade," noting the median SMH semi name is down 22% from highs and only 3 semis are within 10% of their all-time high (DRAM -34%, Micron -29%, Western Digital -36%). Bought Netflix back under $100, expects a solid print (ad tier ~$3B annualized run-rate, subscriber growth intact) and picked it over Disney in his bet with Jenny Harrington (he's currently winning). Featured "Best Stocks in the Market": Target — broke through $120 resistance, posted its first positive comps in 4-5 quarters, guided revenue/EPS higher; sees $150 as more likely than a pullback to $120. Final Trade: Apple, targeting $400, calling it the quiet winner of the AI trade.
- **Shannon Saccocia**: Sees momentum crowding at a 40-year high; argues the "easy button" AI trade (Mag7, then hardware/chips) is over and benefits will now accrue more broadly but modestly across financials, industrials, healthcare over 6-12 months. Downgraded consumer staples to neutral on waning pricing power and rich valuations, not a consumer call. Warned that with earnings expectations so elevated, disappointments (like IBM) risk a long "penalty box" stay, not a V-shaped bounce. Final Trade: Healthcare, as a hedge against a tech selloff and supported by AI productivity tailwinds.
- **Malcolm Etheridge**: Sold IBM after weak preliminary results/AI-book commentary — sees it as dead money for at least a quarter, comparing it to Accenture's slow-motion decline, and flagged ServiceNow/Microsoft as names that could see similar pressure. Rotated into cybersecurity-adjacent thinking (crowdstrike-type beneficiaries) as enterprises redirect budget to securing compute. Bought Cerebras as a speculative "flyer" — high-watermark $175, plans to average down 10% at a time; risk is its customer concentration in OpenAI, whose 2027 contract maturity he worries about. Bought Chubb ahead of Travelers' earnings, betting AI benefits commercial insurers and calling it a good non-tech financial rotation. Final Trade: Rocket Companies (Rocket Mortgage) — cited a positive Morgan Stanley note and sees it as the biggest beneficiary once rates finally move.
- **Jim Lebenthal**: Owns Micron, which he says now only trades in 5%+ daily swings — calling that "not normal." Flagged "technicalities" (not technicals): massive capital raises, elevated margin debt (+55% YoY in the US), and the Korean margin-call blowup (320,000 retail accounts liquidated after leveraged single-stock ETFs were effectively curbed) as signs the market needs a correction. Advised viewers not to chase rotations — stick to your fundamental or technical thesis. On IBM: agreed it's dead money for at least a quarter, comparing it to Cisco's multi-year penalty box, noting IBM's valuation had been unusually rich (20x+ vs. historical 11-13x) and is now down to ~17x. Final Trade: Micron — down 6% today, expects it could reverse up similarly tomorrow given its volatility.
- **Mike Santoli (Senior Markets Commentator)**: Called the market action an "immaculate rotation" — large-cap memory stocks down ~30% in three weeks while the S&P sits within 1% of highs as non-tech sectors and oversold Mag7 names (Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet) pick up the slack. Watching Microsoft/Amazon/Alphabet as they hit falling 50-day moving averages as a key test. Advised staying invested but keeping expectations low given how much good news is already priced in.
- **Oliver Renick (Options Action, from Cboe)**: Screened options data for stocks with unusually expensive out-of-the-money calls (big anticipated upside) relative to their own 52-week history ahead of earnings — top five: Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, Tesla, AMD (only one true chip name). Noted Microsoft, Meta, and Tesla haven't made a new high all year, so bullish options positioning would require real rotation into those names.
- **Final Trades**: Jim Lebenthal — Micron; Malcolm Etheridge — Rocket Companies; Shannon Saccocia — Healthcare; Josh Brown — Apple.
